The Apollo 11 Astronauts

Introducing the Apollo 11 Astronauts

Neil Armstrong

Neil Alden Armstrong was born on August 5th 1930. Born and raised in Ohio he entered Purdue University to study aeronautical engineering, his fees were paid for by the US Navy. Following university, Armstrong became a US Navy Midshipman in 1949 and a Naval Aviator the following year. He joined the NASA Astronaut Corps in 1962 and made his first spaceflight as command pilot of Gemini 8 in March 1966, becoming NASA’s first civilian astronaut to fly in space. The mission had to be aborted after Armstrong was required to use some of his re-entry control fuel to stabilize a dangerous roll caused by a stuck thruster. In 1969 Armstrong became the first man to walk on the moon and his historic words “Its one small step for man, one giant leap for man-kind” were broadcast to an estimated 530 million viewers. After returning to Earth, Armstrong was hailed a hero and took part in many tours and parades across the United States. President Jimmy Carter later presented him with the Congressional Space Medal of Honor in 1978 and he was inducted into the National Aviation Hall of Fame in 1979. In 2009, along with his crewmates, Armstrong received the Congressional Gold Medal.

Buzz Aldrin

Edwin Eugene Aldrin was born on January 20th 1930. He enrolled at West Point Military Academy in 1947, graduating in 1951 and joining the United States Air Force where he learnt to fly fighter planes. In 1959, through the Air Force, he enrolled in the Massachusetts School of Technology and earned a doctorate in Astronautics. His thesis was titled “Line-of-Sight Guidance Techniques for Manned Orbital Rendezvous”. He was initially declined by NASA but earned a spot in the 3rd round of recruitments after the requirements changed to include 1000 hours flying time in a jet aircraft which Aldrin had. Prior to Apollo 11, Aldrin was part of the Gemini 12 mission, Gemini missions having the primary focus of testing different aspects of the flight that Apollo 11 would eventually take. In 1969 Aldrin became the second man to walk on the moon after having landed the lunar module ‘The Eagle’ alongside Neil Armstrong. Along with Armstrong, Aldrin spent an estimated 2 hours on the lunar surface before returning to rendezvous with the Columbia spacecraft and return to Earth. After returning to Earth, Aldrin partook in several tours and appearances around the United States and was also part of the team that designed the Space Shuttle. At the end of the Apollo space program, Aldrin returned to the Air Force where he remained until he retired in 1972.

Michael Collins

Michael Collins was born on October 31st, 1930, in Rome, Italy. His father was serving in the US Military at the time. He attended West Point Military Academy like his father, brother, uncle and cousins, and graduated in 1952. However, he went on to join the Air Force instead of the Army, motivated by wonder of what the next fifty years might bring in aeronautics. Selected as part of NASA’s third group of 14 astronauts in 1963, Collins flew to space once prior to the Apollo 11 mission on Gemini 10, in which he performed orbital rendezvous and two spacewalks. In 1969, whilst Armstrong and Aldrin were carrying out their mission on the moon, Collins orbited the moon 30 times, the second astronaut to have done this alone. Along with Armstrong and Aldrin, Collins was hailed a hero on return to Earth and partook in many parades and tours across the United States. After retiring from NASA in 1970, Collins held several different positions, including Assistant Secretary of State for Public Affairs at the Department of State, director of the National Air and Space Museum, Undersecretary of the Smithsonian Institute and Vice President of LTV Aerospace. He was awarded the Presidential Medal of Freedom in 1969 and later the Congressional Gold Medal in 2011.
